Core-Type (Channel) Induction Furnace

Low-frequency channel furnace • Transformer analogy • I²R heating

Overview

A core-type induction furnace uses electromagnetic induction to melt or hold metal. It is transformer-like: the laminated iron core couples a copper primary winding to a closed loop of molten metal that acts as the secondary. Heat is generated directly in the metal by Joule heating (I²R).

Principle of Operation

Alternating current in the primary winding sets up a time-varying magnetic flux in the laminated core. This links the molten-metal channel (the secondary), inducing a large current. The melt heats as power is dissipated per Joule's law: P = I²R.

P = I2R

Construction

  • Transformer core: Laminated silicon steel to reduce hysteresis and eddy losses.
  • Primary winding: Insulated copper coil, often water/air cooled, fed by 50–60 Hz.
  • Crucible and channel (secondary): Refractory-lined bath with a narrow loop through the core.

Working & Operation

  1. Molten heel: Requires a molten heel to complete the secondary loop at startup.
  2. Electromagnetic stirring: Large currents and fields drive gentle, continuous stirring.
  3. Pinch effect: Excess current density can constrict flow; design limits power density.
  4. Continuous duty: Best as a holding/continuous furnace; cold starts are costly.

Advantages

  • High electrical efficiency; excellent magnetic coupling via iron core.
  • High supply-side power factor (typically 0.8–0.9).
  • Precise temperature control; automatic electromagnetic stirring.
  • Low fume and oxidation; favorable for holding non-ferrous alloys.

Disadvantages

  • Needs molten heel; less flexible for intermittent or multi-alloy work.
  • Channel can clog with slag/inclusions; maintenance required.
  • Pinch-effect limits maximum power density.
  • Higher initial cost due to iron core and construction.

Applications

  • Melting and holding non-ferrous metals (brass, bronze, copper, aluminum alloys, zinc).
  • Holding furnaces in foundries and die-casting lines.
  • Duplexing with other melters (e.g., cupola to channel furnace for holding/trim).

Core-Type vs Coreless Induction Furnace

Feature Core-Type (Channel) Coreless
Core Has laminated iron core No iron core (air-core coupling)
Operating frequency Low (50–60 Hz) Medium/High (≈250 Hz–several kHz)
Power factor High (≈0.8–0.9) Raw low; corrected to near unity via capacitors/inverters
Efficiency Very high (especially for holding) High; depends on size, lining, power density
Starting Needs molten heel Can start from cold solid charge
Stirring Gentle, continuous Vigorous; frequency/power dependent
Flexibility Best for continuous, single-alloy holding Highly flexible across alloys and duty cycles
Applications Holding/melting non-ferrous; duplexing Primary melting of steel, iron, special alloys
